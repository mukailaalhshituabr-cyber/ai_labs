Customer Lifetime Value Analysis - Summary Report
Overview
This analysis examined a dataset of 500 e-commerce customers to understand their behavior patterns and calculate Customer Lifetime Value (CLV). The goal was to identify high-value customer segments and provide actionable business recommendations based on data-driven insights. The analysis combined customer demographics (age and income) with behavioral metrics (purchase frequency and average purchase value) to create a comprehensive view of customer value.

Understanding the CLV Calculation
Customer Lifetime Value was calculated using a formula that considers both purchasing behavior and churn risk. The formula used was: CLV = purchase_frequency × avg_purchase_value × (1 + churn_risk), where churn_risk was defined as 1 minus the ratio of a customer's purchase frequency to the maximum frequency observed in the entire customer base. This approach is particularly insightful because it penalizes customers who make fewer purchases by increasing their churn risk factor, resulting in a more realistic CLV estimate. For example, a customer who purchases infrequently receives a higher churn risk score, which reduces their calculated CLV compared to a frequent purchaser with similar spending habits.

Age Group Segmentation
Customers were divided into four distinct age groups: 18-25, 26-35, 36-50, and 51-70. This segmentation revealed interesting patterns in customer behavior and value distribution. The 51-70 age group emerged as the most valuable segment, with the highest average CLV of approximately $762.69 per customer. Interestingly, this group also represented the largest segment of the customer base with 186 customers, followed closely by the 36-50 age group with 160 customers. The youngest customers (ages 18-25) showed the lowest average CLV at $718.37, suggesting that while they have potential for growth, they currently generate less value compared to older demographics.

Key Statistical Findings
The analysis revealed several important statistical insights about customer behavior. First, the correlation between income and CLV was examined, showing a positive relationship that indicates higher-income customers tend to generate more lifetime value. Second, purchase frequency proved to be a critical driver of CLV, with customers who make more frequent purchases showing substantially higher lifetime values. The top 10% of customers were found to contribute approximately 18.8% of total CLV, demonstrating a moderate concentration of value among high-performing customers. The bottom 50% of customers, conversely, generated only a small fraction of total revenue, highlighting a significant opportunity for improvement through targeted engagement strategies.

Understanding Customer Churn Risk
The churn risk calculation provided valuable insights into customer retention patterns. Churn risk was defined as 1 minus the ratio of a customer's purchase frequency to the maximum frequency in the dataset. This means customers with the highest purchase frequency had a churn risk approaching zero, while those who purchased infrequently showed much higher churn risk scores. This metric is valuable for identifying at-risk customers before they churn, allowing for proactive retention efforts. The analysis showed that customers in the top 10% by CLV had significantly lower churn risk compared to average customers, suggesting that engagement and retention naturally reinforce each other.

Visual Interpretation of the Data
Three visualizations were created to better understand relationships in the data. The scatter plot of income versus CLV, colored by age group, revealed that while income does show some positive relationship with CLV, it is not the sole predictor of customer value. Some customers with moderate incomes achieved high CLV through frequent purchases, while some high-income customers showed lower CLV due to infrequent buying. The bar chart of average CLV by age group clearly illustrated the progression of customer value across age brackets, with older customers generally showing higher value. The correlation heatmap confirmed these observations, showing moderate positive correlations between purchase frequency, income, and CLV, while age showed a weaker but still positive relationship with overall customer value.

Business Recommendations
Based on the findings from this analysis, several actionable recommendations emerge for business strategy. First, implementing a tiered loyalty program specifically targeting the top 10% of customers would help retain the highest-value segment and encourage continued engagement. These customers have already demonstrated their value and are likely to respond positively to recognition and rewards. Second, marketing acquisition campaigns should focus on the 51-70 age group, as this segment naturally demonstrates the highest CLV potential. Third, developing frequency-based incentives, such as "buy 5 get 1 free" programs or subscription models, could help move lower-frequency customers into higher-value categories. Fourth, creating income-based product tiers would allow for personalized upselling strategies - premium products for high-income segments and bundle offers for middle-income segments. Finally, implementing weekly monitoring of churn risk indicators for high-value customers would enable proactive retention efforts before customers disengage.


This customer lifetime value analysis successfully identified key drivers of customer value and highlighted specific segments that deserve focused attention. The combination of demographic and behavioral metrics provided a nuanced understanding of what makes customers valuable over time. The 51-70 age group emerged as the most valuable segment, while the top 10% of customers across all ages represented a significant concentration of total CLV. These insights provide a data-driven foundation for marketing strategy, customer retention efforts, and resource allocation decisions. Future analysis could extend this work by incorporating additional factors such as customer acquisition channel, geographic location, or seasonal purchasing patterns to further refine CLV predictions and personalization strategies.
